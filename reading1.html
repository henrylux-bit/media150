Reading One

Before reading Sontag, I believed photography was not that special because almost everyone has a camera, and since phones are always in our pockets, I felt that anyone could do what I was doing. I also struggled to see my own pictures as art. Photography felt more like a technical skill or a convenient tool than a way of thinking about the world. I cared about composition and light, but I did not fully understand how photographs evoke meaning.

Sontag says that photographs feel like “pieces of reality” rather than interpretations, and when I first read that, it really clicked for me. When I look at a picture, my instinct is to believe, “this is exactly what happened.” But thinking more about it, I’ve realized that a photograph compresses the world into a 2D image. It documents only one angle, one instant in time, and one choice made by the photographer, so what looks like reality is only a piece of it.

One video I saw online really stuck with me. The camera angle and focal length made it look like a man was sprinting toward a woman to rob her. But when more of the clip played, I realized he was actually running to help her. That moment showed me how fast anyone can jump to conclusions just from what we see.

Creating a photo book on Mixbook.com was another time that made me think about photography as an art form. I had to think carefully about sequencing, pacing, and how images worked together. I wondered how a viewer might skim through the book and stop on certain photographs. This process felt closer to storytelling.

My experiences with different photography professors also shaped how I see things. One professor really wanted our photos to look almost like the examples, which pushed me to be precise. Another professor gave instructions but also encouraged us to bring in images we liked and experiment. Going through both approaches helped me realize that photographers are always making choices based on the aesthetic they are looking for.

After watching the docudrama Minamata by Andrew Levitas, I saw more clearly how a photo essay (visual storytelling) can actually shift political outcomes. In the film, a fishing village suffers for years from mercury poisoning while the government and corporations ignore them. What moved things were the photographs taken by W. Eugene Smith. His images made the suffering visible to people who had no idea what was happening. For me, this showed that photography can create public awareness and stir outrage.

Thinking about this today, Sontag’s warning about being addicted to images feels true to me. With things like Meta Ray‑Ban smart glasses, people can record almost all the time, without anyone realizing it. I’ve started to notice how sometimes people help strangers on camera not just out of kindness, but because they want a moment they can post online. That makes the camera feel closer to the “ray gun” Sontag talks about the tool of power that shapes how we act and how we perform in public.

Overall, reading In Plato’s Cave continued to make me realize how powerful and how easily abused photography can be. I still enjoy making images, but I also see that photographs are never neutral. Instead of just “recording” the world, photos help shape how we understand it and how we remember it.
